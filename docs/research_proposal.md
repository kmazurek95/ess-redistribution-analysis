# Research Proposal: Welfare State Institutions, Redistribution Preferences, and AI-Driven Labor Market Transformation in Europe

**Kaleb Mazurek**  
*Prepared in connection with the REPAIR (Re)distributive Politics after the AI Revolution ERC Project, Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam*

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming labor markets across the developed world. Estimates suggest that a substantial share of current employment is susceptible to automation by algorithmic systems (Frey and Osborne, 2017; Acemoglu and Restrepo, 2020), and the displacement effects of these technologies raise profound questions about economic inequality, social protection, and the future of the welfare state (Busemeyer et al., 2022). Yet the consequences of AI-driven labor market change are unlikely to be uniform. Countries differ substantially in their welfare state institutions, labor market regulations, and social protection architectures — and these institutional differences are likely to mediate how automation reshapes employment, inequality, and the political demand for redistribution.

This project investigates the intersection of two questions that are central to contemporary comparative political economy: (1) how do welfare state institutions and labor market regimes shape citizens' preferences for government redistribution of income across European countries, and (2) how might countries' differential exposure to AI-driven automation interact with existing institutional arrangements to reshape the political landscape of redistribution? In addressing these questions, the project replicates and extends an original multilevel analysis of redistribution preferences using European Social Survey data, adding new dimensions of institutional analysis and AI exposure measurement that directly engage with the research agenda of the REPAIR project.

The project makes three contributions. First, it replicates a cross-national multilevel analysis of redistribution preferences across 28 European countries, establishing a baseline understanding of how individual characteristics (income, political trust, meritocratic values) and country-level factors (inequality, GDP) jointly shape attitudes toward redistribution. Second, it extends this analysis by introducing welfare state regime classifications and continuous institutional indicators — employment protection, active labor market policy spending, union density, and social expenditure — to test whether and how institutions mediate the relationship between self-interest and redistribution preferences. Third, it incorporates country-level measures of AI/automation exposure to examine whether countries' "social exposure" to technological displacement shapes the political demand for redistribution, and whether welfare state institutions moderate this effect.

## 2. Research Questions

The project is organized around three interlocking research questions:

**RQ1:** To what extent do individual-level characteristics (income, political trust) and country-level economic conditions (inequality, GDP) explain between-country variation in redistribution preferences across Europe?

**RQ2:** Do welfare state institutions — specifically employment protection legislation, active labor market policies, and collective bargaining structures — mediate the relationship between individual self-interest and support for redistribution? In other words, do stronger institutional frameworks weaken the negative effect of income on redistribution support?

**RQ3:** Does country-level exposure to AI-driven automation independently shape redistribution preferences, and do welfare state institutions moderate this relationship — such that countries with stronger social protection and reskilling infrastructure show different preference patterns from those without?

## 3. Literature Review

### 3.1 Determinants of Redistribution Preferences

Research on redistribution preferences has a long lineage in political economy. The foundational Meltzer-Richard (1981) model predicts that demand for redistribution increases when a voter's income falls below the mean, generating a straightforward self-interest logic: poorer citizens favor redistribution because they are net beneficiaries, while wealthier citizens oppose it because they are net contributors. Extensive empirical work broadly confirms the negative relationship between income and redistribution support (Corneo and Grüner, 2002; Guillaud, 2013), although the magnitude of this effect varies considerably across national contexts.

Beyond material self-interest, values and beliefs play a significant role. Individuals who hold meritocratic convictions — that economic outcomes are primarily a function of effort rather than circumstance — tend to oppose redistribution (Corneo and Grüner, 2002; Fong, 2001). Political trust also shapes preferences, though in complex ways. Macdonald (2020) demonstrates that trust in government institutions is associated with greater willingness to accept redistribution, as trusting citizens are less concerned about misuse of public funds. However, Rudolph and Evans (2005) argue that political trust can also be conditional on limited government, producing a negative association between trust and support for expanded state intervention.

Crucially, redistribution preferences are not formed in a vacuum. They are embedded in institutional and economic contexts that shape both the risks individuals face and the policy responses they consider plausible (Svallfors, 1997; Rehm, 2009). Comparative research using the European Social Survey has documented substantial cross-national variation in redistribution support (Olivera, 2015; Pittau, Massari, and Zelli, 2013). Explaining this variation requires attention not only to compositional differences in individual characteristics across countries but also to contextual factors — including inequality, wealth, and institutional structures — that condition how preferences are formed.

### 3.2 Welfare State Regimes and Institutional Mediation

The comparative welfare state literature, anchored by Esping-Andersen's (1990) seminal typology of welfare capitalism, provides a powerful framework for understanding cross-national institutional variation. The original three-worlds typology — distinguishing social-democratic, conservative-corporatist, and liberal regimes — has been extended by subsequent scholars to include Mediterranean (Ferrera, 1996) and post-communist varieties (Cerami and Vanhuysse, 2009). The complementary Varieties of Capitalism framework (Hall and Soskice, 2001) highlights a parallel institutional distinction between liberal and coordinated market economies, with particular emphasis on how labor market institutions, vocational training systems, and industrial relations regimes coordinate economic activity differently.

These institutional frameworks are directly relevant to redistribution preferences because they shape the risks individuals face and the policy instruments available to manage them. In social-democratic welfare states, generous universal social protection and strong active labor market policies (ALMP) may reduce economic insecurity across the income distribution, potentially weakening the self-interest effect on redistribution preferences. In liberal welfare states, where social protection is more residual and market-dependent, economic vulnerability may sharpen the association between income and redistribution support. Busemeyer and Tober (2023) provide evidence consistent with this logic, finding that individuals in more generous welfare systems tend to express less anxiety about technological change — suggesting that institutional context shapes not only policy preferences but also risk perceptions.

Specific institutional mechanisms deserve attention. Employment protection legislation (EPL) governs the ease with which firms can dismiss workers, shaping the immediate displacement risk from technological adoption (Alesina et al., 2018). Active labor market policies encompass reskilling and retraining programs that facilitate labor market transitions (Eichhorst, Hemerijck, and Scalise, 2022). Union density and collective bargaining coverage determine workers' collective capacity to negotiate the terms of technological adoption at the firm and sectoral levels. Social expenditure as a share of GDP captures the overall generosity of the social safety net. Each of these institutional dimensions may independently mediate how economic self-interest translates into redistribution preferences.

### 3.3 Automation, AI, and Welfare State Politics

A rapidly growing literature examines how automation and AI-driven technological change reshape welfare state politics. The pioneering work of Thewissen and Rueda (2019) establishes a direct empirical link between individual-level automation risk and redistribution preferences, demonstrating that workers in routine task-intensive occupations — those most susceptible to technological displacement — express significantly stronger support for government redistribution. They frame this as an insurance logic: individuals facing elevated future income risk demand greater social protection. Importantly, they find this effect is stronger among higher-income workers in routine occupations, who have more to lose from displacement.

Subsequent research has expanded this agenda in important directions. Busemeyer and Sahm (2022) distinguish between support for redistribution, social investment, and universal basic income, finding that automation risk is positively associated with redistribution support but negatively associated with support for social investment policies — suggesting that individuals facing immediate displacement threats may prefer compensatory over prospective policy responses. Dermont and Weisstanner (2020) similarly explore whether basic income enjoys support as a response to automation, finding surprisingly weak associations. Kurer and Häusermann (2022) demonstrate that automation risk affects not only policy preferences but also political participation, with implications for how technological change reshapes democratic politics.

The institutional context of these preference dynamics has received increasing theoretical attention but requires further empirical investigation. Busemeyer (2022) argues that digitalization and the welfare state are locked in a bidirectional relationship: welfare institutions shape how technological change affects workers, while technological change simultaneously creates pressure for institutional adaptation. The edited volume by Busemeyer, Kemmerling, van Kersbergen, and Marx (2022) provides the most comprehensive treatment to date, documenting how digitalization challenges existing welfare state arrangements across multiple policy domains. As they note, a key unresolved question is whether the current wave of technological change represents a fundamentally new challenge requiring paradigmatic welfare state transformation, or whether existing institutional frameworks can accommodate these pressures through incremental adaptation.

This is precisely the research gap that the present project addresses. While existing studies have documented individual-level associations between automation risk and redistribution preferences, and others have theorized about institutional mediation, comparatively little work has combined both levels of analysis in a single empirical framework. Specifically, no study to date has tested cross-level interactions between country-level AI exposure and welfare state institutions in shaping redistribution attitudes — the analytical approach this project pursues.

### 3.4 The Concept of Social Exposure

A key conceptual contribution of this project — and one directly aligned with the REPAIR project's framework — is the distinction between technical exposure and social exposure to AI-driven automation. Technical exposure refers to the objective susceptibility of a country's occupational structure to automation, as measured by indicators derived from Frey and Osborne (2017) or the OECD AI exposure indices. Social exposure, by contrast, captures the degree to which a country's institutional architecture leaves workers and communities vulnerable to the disruptive effects of automation. A country with high technical exposure but robust employment protection, generous reskilling infrastructure, and strong collective bargaining may experience automation as a manageable transition rather than a social crisis. Conversely, a country with lower technical exposure but weak institutional buffers may find even modest automation shocks politically destabilizing.

This distinction draws on the broader insight from comparative political economy that economic shocks are always institutionally mediated (Scharpf, 2000; Thelen, 2014). The REPAIR project's focus on how "welfare state institutions and welfare politics shape political and societal responses to AI-driven labor market transformation" reflects precisely this perspective. The present project operationalizes social exposure as a composite of technical AI exposure and institutional capacity (inverse of EPL, ALMP spending, social expenditure), enabling cross-national comparison of countries' differential vulnerability to AI-driven labor market change.

## 4. Theoretical Framework and Hypotheses

The theoretical framework integrates three strands of reasoning: (1) the self-interest model of redistribution preferences, (2) the institutional mediation thesis from comparative welfare state research, and (3) the emerging literature on automation and welfare state politics. The core argument is that welfare state institutions do not merely respond to citizen preferences — they actively shape how economic self-interest and technological risk translate into policy demands.

From this framework, the following hypotheses are derived:

**Replication hypotheses (from original analysis):**

- H1: Higher individual income is associated with weaker support for redistribution.
- H2: Higher political trust is associated with stronger support for redistribution.
- H3: Country-level inequality (Gini coefficient) amplifies the negative effect of income on redistribution support (cross-level interaction).

**Institutional mediation hypotheses:**

- H4: Support for redistribution varies systematically across welfare state regimes, with social-democratic regimes showing lower average support (reflecting existing high levels of protection) and post-communist regimes showing higher average support.
- H5: The negative effect of income on redistribution support is weaker in countries with stronger employment protection and active labor market policies (institutional buffering).
- H6: The negative effect of income on redistribution support is weaker in countries with higher union density and collective bargaining coverage.

**AI exposure hypotheses:**

- H7: Higher country-level AI/automation exposure is associated with stronger average support for redistribution. *[Tested and rejected: coef = -0.014, p = 0.857. The direct pathway from AI exposure to redistribution preferences is not supported.]*
- H8: Welfare state institutions moderate the relationship between AI exposure and redistribution preferences: in countries with strong social protection, AI exposure has a weaker effect on redistribution demand than in countries with weak institutional buffers. *[Tested and rejected: all AI x regime interactions are non-significant.]*

**Indirect channel hypothesis (post-hoc, supported by cross-level interaction results):**

- H9: AI-driven labor market restructuring affects redistribution preferences indirectly, by reshaping income distributions and increasing inequality (empirical support: Minniti, Prettner & Venturini, 2025, find AI innovation reduces labor share by 0.5-1.6% in European regions; Almusharraf, 2025, estimates automation contributed to a 12% rise in the Gini index across 2000-2023). The significant income x Gini interaction (H3, p = 0.002) identifies the channel through which inequality shocks — including those driven by AI adoption — propagate into nonlinear attitude dynamics. This indirect pathway motivates the agent-based simulation extension.

## 5. Data and Methods

### 5.1 Data Sources

The analysis draws on multiple data sources:

**Individual-level data:** European Social Survey Round 9 (2018), covering approximately 36,000 respondents across 28 European countries (Slovakia excluded due to missing macro-level data). The ESS provides the dependent variable (support for government redistribution of income, reverse-coded to a 1–5 scale), key individual-level predictors (household income decile, trust in parliament, age, gender, education, unemployment history), and meritocratic values items.

**Country-level economic data:** Gini coefficient, GDP, and unemployment rate for 2018, sourced from the OECD Income Distribution Database, OECD National Accounts, and OECD Employment Database respectively. These replicate the original analysis.

**Institutional data:** Employment Protection Legislation strictness (OECD), Active Labor Market Policy spending as a percentage of GDP (OECD Social Expenditure Database), union density (OECD/ICTWSS Database), collective bargaining coverage (ICTWSS), and total social spending as a percentage of GDP (OECD SOCX). All indicators target 2018 or the nearest available year.

**AI/automation exposure data:** Country-level AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE) scores from Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2021), aggregated from 774 SOC 6-digit occupations to country level using a SOC-to-ISCO-08 crosswalk and Eurostat Labour Force Survey (2018) employment weights. The Felten AIOE measures overlap between occupational task content and demonstrated AI capabilities — capturing cognitive/professional exposure rather than manufacturing automation risk. A composite "social exposure" measure is constructed combining technical exposure with inverse institutional capacity. See `scripts/aggregate_aioe.py` for the full reproducible aggregation pipeline.

### 5.2 Analytical Approach

The analysis employs multilevel (hierarchical) linear models appropriate for the nested data structure (individuals within countries). The modeling strategy proceeds in stages:

**Stage 1: Replication (Models 1–7).** A seven-model sequence replicates the original analysis. An empty model establishes the intraclass correlation (ICC), confirming meaningful between-country variation. Subsequent models add individual-level predictors, country-level economic variables, random slopes for income and political trust, and cross-level interactions (income × Gini, political trust × GDP). This stage validates the baseline findings using Python (pymer4) rather than the original R implementation.

**Stage 2: Institutional extension (Models 8–13).** Welfare regime dummies (Esping-Andersen extended typology) replace the country-level economic variables, followed by models testing income × regime interactions. Then, continuous institutional indicators (EPL, ALMP, union density, social spending) replace the regime dummies, with cross-level interactions testing whether these institutions moderate the income-redistribution relationship (H5, H6).

**Stage 3: AI exposure extension (Models 14–16).** Country-level AI exposure (Felten AIOE) enters as a Level-2 predictor, followed by interactions between AI exposure and welfare regime, and a social exposure composite. This stage tests whether AI exposure directly shapes redistribution preferences (H7) and whether institutional context moderates this relationship (H8). Results: H7 and H8 are rejected — AI exposure shows no significant direct or moderated effect on redistribution preferences (all p > 0.4). This null finding redirects theoretical attention to the indirect channel: AI reshapes inequality, which activates the income x Gini interaction identified in Stage 1.

**Stage 4: Agent-based simulation.** Given the null direct effect of AI exposure, the simulation models the indirect channel: AI-driven labor displacement increases inequality (Gini shock), which activates the empirically estimated income x Gini interaction, producing nonlinear attitude dynamics. The simulation is parameterized by the regression coefficients from Stage 1 and country-level data, enabling exploration of tipping thresholds across welfare regime types. This approach bridges the cross-national statistical analysis and the forward-looking scenario analysis central to the REPAIR agenda.

All models use REML estimation and robust standard errors to address heteroscedasticity. Level-1 variables are grand-mean centered, and Level-2 variables are standardized. Variance Inflation Factor tests assess multicollinearity. Missing institutional data for non-OECD countries (e.g., Montenegro, Serbia) are handled through subsample analyses.

### 5.3 Welfare Regime Classifications

Two complementary classification schemes are employed. The Esping-Andersen extended typology distinguishes social-democratic (DK, SE, NO, FI), conservative-corporatist (DE, FR, BE, AT, NL, CH), liberal (GB, IE), Mediterranean (ES, IT, PT, CY), and post-communist (CZ, EE, HR, HU, LT, LV, PL, SI, BG, RS, ME) regimes. The Varieties of Capitalism framework (Hall and Soskice, 2001) provides an alternative classification into liberal market economies, coordinated market economies, Mediterranean market economies, and dependent market economies. Using both frameworks enhances the robustness of institutional findings and permits comparison of their explanatory power.

## 6. Expected Contributions and Relevance to the REPAIR Project

This project is designed to demonstrate both substantive alignment and methodological readiness for the REPAIR PhD position. Each analytical component maps directly to the project's objectives:

The **replication analysis** demonstrates proficiency in multilevel modeling and cross-national comparative research — core methodological requirements of the PhD position. The **institutional extension** directly engages with Objective 1 of the REPAIR project (analyzing how different welfare state and labor market regimes are affected by AI-driven automation) and with Objective 4 (developing new theoretical understanding of welfare state adaptation). The construction of comparative institutional indicators and country typologies mirrors the PhD's stated task of developing "comparative indicators or typologies of countries." The **AI exposure extension** tests the direct "technological threat" hypothesis and finds it unsupported (p = 0.857). This null finding is itself informative: it rules out the naive pathway from AI exposure to redistribution demand and redirects attention to the indirect channel through inequality. The significant income x Gini interaction (p = 0.002) identifies the empirical mechanism through which AI-driven economic restructuring could propagate into attitude change — not through direct perception of technological threat, but through AI's downstream effects on income distributions.

The agent-based simulation extension operationalizes this indirect channel, using the empirically estimated coefficients to explore when and where inequality shocks produce nonlinear tipping in redistribution preferences across welfare regime types. This bridges the REPAIR project's interest in both empirical analysis and forward-looking policy scenarios.

## 7. Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the cross-sectional nature of the ESS data limits causal inference; future work using ESS Rounds 10 (2020) and 11 (2022/23) could exploit temporal variation in both automation exposure and redistribution preferences, including the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic on welfare state attitudes. Second, country-level AI exposure measures remain imperfect — they capture susceptibility to automation rather than actual displacement, and the aggregation of occupation-level risk to country level obscures within-country heterogeneity. Third, the small number of Level-2 units (28 countries) limits the number of country-level predictors that can be simultaneously included in multilevel models, requiring careful model specification.

These limitations also point toward the value of the mixed-methods approach envisioned by the REPAIR project. The quantitative typology constructed here can guide the selection of ideal-type cases for in-depth qualitative institutional analysis — for instance, comparing a social-democratic country with high AI exposure but strong institutional buffers to a post-communist country with comparable exposure but weaker institutional capacity. Such case comparison would illuminate the micro-level mechanisms through which welfare state institutions mediate responses to technological change, complementing the macro-level patterns identified through multilevel modeling.
